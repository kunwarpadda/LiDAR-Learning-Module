How LiDAR Measures the World

From laser pulses to 3D maps

Module Overview

Laser measurements become 3D maps when distance, position, and orientation work together.

What you will learn

  1. Explain what LiDAR is and how it differs from a regular camera.
  2. Describe how time-of-flight can be used to estimate distance.
  3. Identify how GNSS/GPS and IMU data place LiDAR measurements into a coordinate system.
  4. Explain what a point cloud is and how it represents a real-world surface or object.
  5. Identify possible sources of error in LiDAR data collection.
  6. Explain why small errors can affect the accuracy of a final 3D map.
  7. Apply your understanding to a real-world scenario where LiDAR accuracy matters.

Essential questions

  1. How can a laser mounted on an aircraft measure the shape of the Earth?
  2. How are millions of individual laser measurements transformed into a 3D point cloud?
  3. Why can small measurement errors affect the reliability of a final 3D map?

Section 1

LiDAR or Camera?

Cameras record light and color from a scene. LiDAR actively sends laser pulses and measures distance, so it can represent depth even when the final display looks like an image.

Section 2

Measuring Distance with Light

LiDAR sends out a laser pulse and measures how long it takes to return after hitting a surface. Because the speed of light is known, travel time can be converted into distance.

The idea

The laser pulse makes a round trip: sensor to surface, then surface back to sensor. LiDAR measures that total travel time.

distance = (speed of light × round-trip time) ÷ 2
  1. Send a pulse of light.
  2. Measure how long it takes to return.
  3. Use the speed of light to estimate total travel distance.
  4. Divide by two to get the one-way distance to the surface.

Section 3

From Measurements to Point Clouds

A single LiDAR measurement gives one distance. A LiDAR system collects many measurements very quickly. When measurements are combined with sensor position and orientation information, they can be placed into a 3D coordinate system.

Activity draft

Build a mini point cloud using a simple grid.

Check-in: Why does a point cloud need many points instead of just one?

Section 4

Why Small Errors Matter

LiDAR data is powerful, but it is not automatically perfect. Errors in sensor position, angle, calibration, or timing can shift where points appear in the final map.

Activity draft

Compare one accurate point-cloud scenario with another scenario that includes a small shift.

Reflection: If this map were being used for flood mapping, why would the error matter?

Section 5

Real-World LiDAR Case Study

LiDAR is used in flood mapping, forestry, urban planning, archaeology, infrastructure inspection, and autonomous vehicles.
