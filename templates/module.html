Glossary — key terms in plain language
LiDAR
Light Detection and Ranging — a sensor that sends out laser pulses and measures distance from how long they take to come back.
Point cloud
A set of many measured 3D points that together outline the shape of a surface or object.
Time-of-flight
Using the travel time of a laser pulse to work out how far away a surface is.
GNSS / GPS
Global Navigation Satellite System — tells the sensor where it is on the Earth. GPS is one example of a GNSS.
IMU
Inertial Measurement Unit — tells the sensor which way it is tilted or pointing.
Coordinate system
A shared 3D grid (x, y, z) that every measured point is placed into so the points line up correctly.
Calibration
Adjusting the sensors so their measurements line up accurately. Poor calibration shifts points in the final map.

LiDAR or Camera?

Cameras record light and color from a scene. A LiDAR sensor — often mounted on an aircraft, drone, or vehicle — actively sends out laser pulses and measures distance, so it can represent depth even when the final display looks like an image.

Section 2

Measuring Distance with Light

LiDAR sends out a laser pulse and measures how long it takes to return after hitting a surface. Because the speed of light is known, travel time can be converted into distance.

The idea

The laser pulse makes a round trip: sensor to surface, then surface back to sensor. LiDAR measures that total travel time.

distance = (speed of light × round-trip time) ÷ 2
  1. Send a pulse of light.
  2. Measure how long it takes to return.
  3. Use the speed of light to estimate total travel distance.
  4. Divide by two to get the one-way distance to the surface.

Section 3

From Measurements to Point Clouds

A single LiDAR measurement gives one distance. A LiDAR system collects many of these measurements very quickly. To turn them into a useful 3D map, the system also has to know two things for every pulse: where the sensor is and which way it is pointing.

How LiDAR knows where it is

Each laser pulse only measures a distance. To place that measurement in the real world, the sensor combines it with two other instruments:

  • GNSS / GPS (Global Navigation Satellite System) tells the sensor where it is — its position over the Earth. GPS is one example of a GNSS.
  • IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit) tells the sensor which way it is tilted or pointing — its orientation as an aircraft pitches and rolls.

Distance (from time-of-flight), position (from GNSS), and orientation (from the IMU) are combined to place every point into a shared coordinate system: a 3D grid of x, y, and z values that all the points use so they line up correctly. Section 4

Why Small Errors Matter

LiDAR data is powerful, but it is not automatically perfect. Small errors in the sensor's position (from GNSS), its orientation (from the IMU), its calibration (how well the instruments are tuned to agree with each other), or its timing can each shift where a point lands in the final map.

Range is how far each pulse travels to the ground — about 80 m in this airborne example. A small tilt (IMU error) turns into a bigger position error the farther the pulse travels, which is why the IMU shift is measured at 80 m.
